The harder question follows the launch

The February announcement was clear. Client 360, CitiScribe, AskWealth CIO, and the portfolio-insights tool reach full Citi Private Bank rollout in April. Four tools, one connected thesis, and a timeline. The harder question follows the launch: which advisor workflows absorb the tools quickly, which require an enablement layer, and which sit on a layer of the stack where an LLM is the wrong answer. The Miami desk, with its cross-border book and its different compliance cadence, is the desk where that question is loudest.

How we think about wealth-desk workflows

We think about wealth-desk workflows in thirds. Roughly 60% is traditional code and database work, the kind of data plumbing that Client 360 is already addressing. Roughly 30% is rule-based logic: FATCA triggers, CRS classification, Reg BI file formatting, periodic review cadence. Roughly 10% is a genuine LLM problem, the unstructured, contextual reasoning that CitiScribe and AskWealth CIO actually serve.

Teams that try to run all three on an LLM spend a lot of budget, move slowly through Model Risk review, and produce outputs that a banker will not trust. Our engagement is short and practical: map one high-volume Miami workflow to those three layers, document what belongs where, and hand your Wealth Technology team a specification that clears Third Party Risk Management faster than an abstract AI roadmap would.

The relevant paper

Our Ethics Engine paper, a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs, documents a method for assessing model behavior against stated institutional values. Useful input for Model Risk Management reviews under SR 11-7. Published on arXiv at arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742, with the open-source audit engine at github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine.
